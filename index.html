What's inside

Key Features

A fully interactive 3D solar system built with Python, PyOpenGL, and GLUT — rendered in real time on your machine.

🌟

OpenGL Lighting

Point-light source at the Sun position with warm diffuse, ambient fill, and specular highlights on all planetary surfaces.

🪐

Saturn's Rings

Multi-band ring system with a Cassini Division gap, semi-transparent blending, and correct axial tilt inheritance.

🌍

Earth's Moon

The Moon orbits Earth in real time, staying within Earth's tilted equatorial frame independently of the planet's self-spin.

🌌

Deep Space Sky

600 size-varied stars with colour tints, 5 coloured nebula clusters, and a pre-generated asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.

🎥

Free Camera

Full spherical orbit camera — pan, tilt, zoom, and jump to any planet from the right-click context menu.

⚡

60 fps Animation

Frame-rate independent delta-time physics keeps orbits and spins smooth at any display refresh rate.

🖱️ Mouse & Menu

🖱️ Right Click — Scene Menu

Right-click anywhere in the window to open the context menu.

  • Focus Planet — jump camera to any of the 8 planets
  • Change Speed — Slow (0.3×) · Normal (1×) · Fast (3×)
  • Toggle Labels — show or hide planet name overlays
  • Reset View — return to the default camera angle
  • Exit — close the simulation cleanly
🪐 Focus Planet

Select any planet from the right-click submenu to zoom the camera directly toward it at the right distance and angle.

Focus Planet ▶  Mercury · Venus · Earth · Mars · Jupiter · Saturn · Uranus · Neptune
⚡ Speed Control

Adjust the global orbit speed from the Speed submenu at any time — even while the simulation is running.

  • Slow (0.3×) — ideal for close-up observation
  • Normal (1×) — default real-time simulation rate
  • Fast (3×) — watch full planetary orbits quickly

About the Project

. The project demonstrates core Computer Graphics concepts including 3D transformations, OpenGL lighting models, blending, and interactive camera control.

All 8 planets orbit with realistic axial tilts, self-rotation speeds, atmospheric glow layers, and a physically-derived camera that uses spherical coordinates to orbit freely around the solar system.
